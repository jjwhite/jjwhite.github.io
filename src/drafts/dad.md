My father took his own life at 36.  My older brother took his at 45.  In just a few months I will have outlived them both which is a very strange feeling. About a year ago, while helping my mom move, I uncovered some of my dad's old journals. I read them at the time and then put them away.  Recently, however, I pulled them back out and read through them again.  I hoped that reading through them again might help me understand what he went through.  Maybe I could write about what I found and help others.  Maybe I can find my brother's journals and look for similarities.  

One thing that struck me as I read is that where I had originally read them as if I was still that little boy who lost his dad, this time I read it as someone who maybe could have been a mentor or sounding board for him. (This was admittedly a very odd experience)

To start, here are some things that jumped out at me:

- He was on numerous drugs, seemingly all at the same time: Desaryl, Amitriptolene, and Xanax

- He was seeing a therapist

- Despite this, it really reads like he's fighting this all alone. He mentions books he was reading and he seemed to be caught in an infinite cycle of intellectualization of his issues.  In many ways, it was this never-ending intellectualization that was what was haunting him the most

- If I was going to offer him advice, I would have recommended CodA or some other 12 step program. It feels like he was just thinking in circles and medicating in an effort to get out of his own head.  I feel he could have benefited greatly from a support system, and a practical step-by-step program that could keep his focus.  

- Multiple mentions of a "dreamlike" reality or out-of-body like experiences (this is something that my nephew and my son have mentioned as well)

- There were so many things that I could relate to and have felt myself:
  - He disliked sunny days because he felt pressure to get things done when maybe all he wanted to do was relax
  - Guilt when relaxing
  - Difficulty making decisions on how to spend his time when he had "idle" time


In my young adulthood I can remember feeling like I missed out on so much learning from my dad.  He could fix anything, seemed to just know how things worked.  Yet, reading his thoughts and he had basically the exact opposite feeling about himself.  Feeling out-of-place and not nearly as good as everyone else.

One thing I had always said about my brother is that I thought he had this "ideal image" in his head about what his life was supposed to look like or the man he was "supposed" to be.  I found it fascinating, then to find this quote in my dad's journal:

"maybe somewhere inside I have this picture of the ideal father and husband and son and since I am unable to achieve or live upto these images, my shame and embarassment cause me to dislike myself"

We often picture depression as this deep, dark, pit that someone gets stuck in and cannot escape.  What I read in my dad's journal is different.  If reads more like a relentless assault.  An ever-intensifying pressure that was slowly squeezing the joy of life out of him.  We tend to also think of it as big (existential) problems but that wasn't the case either.  It was seeminly simple things like: not feeling up to go out and mow the lawn.  Feeling guilty for relaxing on a beautiful day. 

It reads like someone who struggled to accept life as it came.  It was a control problem?  Always striving for some indescript perfect life.  Constant comparison.  I can relate to this.  I know I have never felt a pressure so crushing as to contemplate suicide, but almost everything he writes about I have experienced to some degree.  And that might be the trick isn't it?  It's all the same shit.  But some of us experience it to such a stronger degree that it takes over our thinking.  It seeps it's weay into every crack and crevice of our existance until that's all we feel.

That's not to say that I couldn't see myself ever getting there.  I got to the point where I felt like I was starting to understand how someone could get there.  Thankfully, I was able to do some work on myself via therapy, and Coda (admittedly I did NOT finish the 12 steps which I still hope to do)  But even my short time in Coda really helped me with acceptance and letting go of a lot of my control addictions.
